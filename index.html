Engineering Philosophy

How I build

I build systems for operators who can’t afford downtime — maritime vessels mid-voyage, logistics fleets crossing borders, enterprise teams managing millions in asset value. That context shapes how I write code: observable by default, failure-tolerant, and comprehensible to the engineer who inherits it at 2am.

I believe the best software comes from engineers who understand the domain deeply enough to push back on requirements. After five years in maritime technology, I understand voyage economics, regulatory compliance pressure, and the operational reality of decisions made at sea — and I bring that into every architecture decision I make.

I keep three questions in mind before shipping anything: Can we debug this in production? Can we hand this to a new engineer in six months? Does it solve the actual problem or the stated problem? If the answer to any of those is no, we’re not done yet.

Domain depth over framework breadth
Understanding maritime operations made me a better engineer than knowing every framework. I’d rather deeply know one domain than shallowly touch ten.
Observability is not optional
Production systems must be debuggable. Logging, tracing, and health metrics are first-class features — not post-launch additions.
Simplicity is the senior skill
The most complex systems I’ve seen were made complex by engineers who wanted to appear clever. The hardest thing to write is a system your team can actually maintain.
